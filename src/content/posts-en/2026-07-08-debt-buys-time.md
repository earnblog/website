In the first five months of this year, the five largest cloud and AI giants issued $159 billion in bonds — 47% more than the same window last year. Some analysts estimate the group's public debt could stack up to $230–$240 billion by year-end. Meanwhile, the credit spreads on those bonds are quietly widening, already stretched to levels that say "the rating ought to be a notch lower." The market's reading is decorous: the giants' balance sheets are healthy, and issuing debt is a mature move — smart use of cheap capital to accelerate the land grab.

But the act of issuing debt says something the keynote never will.

What a company expands *on* determines the nature of the expansion. Expanding on the cash flow you earned is investment — today's profit buying tomorrow's capacity. Expanding on borrowed money, whose interest depends on a batch of revenue that "has not appeared yet," is no longer investment. It is discounting a future not yet arrived and plugging it into a hole in the present.

Start with how large the gap is. The major giants' combined annual AI capex runs about $400 billion, while the real AI revenue you can match against it is roughly $100 billion. That three-hundred-billion gap was, until recently, absorbed by internal cash flow; increasingly it is being filled by the bond market. That is the truth of the issuance wave: not that money is so cheap they can't resist borrowing, but that their own water can no longer irrigate a field this size.

Borrowing itself is not the danger. The danger is that what the borrowing buys is not what you actually need. Debt can buy chips, buy power, buy data centers — the one thing it cannot buy is the *return* on compute. That return has to grow out of real end demand, in hard cash, on its own schedule, and that schedule cannot be rushed. So what debt buys here is not return; it is time — the stretch of time in which you can keep expanding and keep telling the story before the return materializes. You are wagering interest you must certainly pay against a cash flow that may not certainly come.

This is where "credit is part of price" cuts sharpest. Capex looks like spending money on capacity; underneath, it is injecting credit into the entire AI narrative. Debt-funded expansion upgrades that credit from "your own money" to "the whole bond market's money": the scale of your expansion is no longer bounded by what you earned, but by how much the market is still willing to lend you. It is a textbook reflexive loop — debt props up capex, capex props up the growth narrative, and the growth narrative in turn emboldens the market to keep lending. Left foot on right foot: the more you borrow the bolder you expand, the more you expand the more you must borrow. On the way up, the loop self-reinforces, lovely as a perpetual-motion machine.

The trouble is that the fuel of this machine is not growth — it is "the market's willingness to lend." And that willingness is the least stable link in the whole loop, and it is written in the credit spread. A widening spread is the bond market's first no-vote, cast in hard cash: the same debt now needs a higher coupon to clear, which means lenders have started to *price* the risk that the return arrives too late. The tide has not gone out, but ripples have crossed the surface. The vicious part of a reflexive loop is that it runs backward exactly as fast as it ran forward: once the market tightens lending, capex is forced to contract, the growth narrative caves, the caving narrative widens the spread further, the cost of rolling old debt into new rises again — the same loop, run in reverse, turns from a perpetual-motion machine into a noose.

Run it through the risk-reward lattice and the arithmetic turns colder. A company expanding on cash flow has upside of growth and downside of, at worst, slower growth — the downside has a floor. A company expanding on debt, with the return not yet realized, still has the same upside of growth, but its downside gains an extra layer: even if the business merely "fails to deteriorate, just slows a little," the interest is still due, the maturing debt still comes due, and the door to roll old into new may already have narrowed because the spread widened. The upside did not get bigger; the downside acquired a hard constraint. That is the asymmetry of leverage — it does not change the direction of your bet, it only magnifies the cost of being wrong. Picking up a cheap cigar butt already means sealed upside and open downside; adding debt leverage to chase an unrealized narrative is voluntarily prying that downside wider.

Early and late mirror each other here too. Early in a healthy expansion, cash flow, real demand, and moderate borrowing push capacity up together — several real inputs converging into one rising curve. Late in it, real demand's contribution has topped out, yet the curve still wants to climb, and the only fuel left to splice on is "borrow a little more." When expansion is driven more and more by debt rather than cash flow, the curve has degenerated from "reality pushing" into "credit propping." While it holds, everyone is happy; when it stops holding, the first thing to snap is always the capacity at the far end, held up by borrowed time.

So what deserves watching is never how much cash the giants have on the books or how large their market caps are — it is which way the ratio of cash flow to debt is moving inside this expansion. The more a thing has to be sustained on "borrowed time," the closer it drifts to the critical point: the return must arrive before the time runs out, or the borrowed time will be reclaimed, principal and interest, all at once.

Debt cannot buy the return on compute. It can only extend the term you spend waiting for it. And what the market ultimately settles is never how much time you borrowed — it is whether, before that time ran out, the real cash flow ever showed up.

<p class="kicker">Expand on cash flow and you buy capacity; expand on debt and you buy time — and time is the one thing in this world you can borrow and never repay.</p>
