Earnings pre-announcements are stacking up today, and optical communications is the brightest beam of all. Yangtze Optical guided first-half net profit of 2.4 to 3.0 billion yuan — up 711% to 914% year on year; Dongshan Precision guided up nearly 300%; PCB leaders like Wus and Shengyi locked limit-up in the afternoon. A near-unquestionable narrative flooded in behind it: compute demand is exploding, and optical modules, fiber, and PCB are the most certain shovel-sellers of this AI build-out — the results are already in, and the secular bull is only beginning. The numbers are so staggering, the logic so smooth, that charging in almost feels like the rational move.

But let me say the unwelcome thing: **the moment the growth rate is fastest is usually the moment the risk-reward is worst.** The more that 711% quickens your pulse, the more you should be wary — what you're chasing isn't growth, it's a position whose upside has already been spent by that very number, and whose downside has only just swung open. This is not a bearish call on the optical business; it's a bearish call on the *act* of charging in holding the hottest growth rate. Those two things are separated by an entire margin of safety.

To make this clear, first pry apart two words people blur every day: *growth rate* and *growth*. What the market is chasing is that **growth rate** of 711% — but what actually constitutes a margin of safety was never the rate; it is **growth** — whether a business can keep compounding its profits, year after year, for many years. A growth rate is first-order, instantaneous, mean-reverting; growth is second-order, durable, able to ride through cycles. A number like 711% is pulse-shaped by nature: half of it comes from the concentrated burst of compute capex over this year or two, the other half from a base last year that had collapsed into the dust. It describes "how fierce this instant is," not "how long the fierceness lasts." Mistaking the height of a single pulse for the slope of a compounding curve is the most classic — and most expensive — misreading on every cyclical growth stock.

Set it in the risk-reward lattice and the picture turns colder. Whether any business is worth buying comes down, in the end, to two things: how much upside is left, and how wide the downside gapes. When a company's profit growth spikes to 711% and the stock locks limit-up, its **upside** has already been largely spent — right there in the process of that number being published and chased — under four words: *results already delivered.* You paid full fare, or a premium, for growth that has already happened. And its **downside**? Precisely because this round's capex capacity is still being laid down, still ramping, not yet released, it is wide open. Upside capped by realized results, downside gaping with incoming capacity — this is the ugliest structure in the whole lattice: concavity. Returns capped on the way up, risk open on the way down. You picked the priciest cigar butt of the season and mistook it for a growth stock.

And optical communications happens to be the most convex link of all — the one where the bullwhip cracks hardest. Its capacity is built in years — drawing fiber, adding lines, installing equipment; the compute demand it faces changes face by the quarter, even by the month. Between them sits a long supply chain: from hyperscaler capex, to module orders, to fiber, to PCB. A tiny twitch in end demand gets amplified link by link, raising towering waves upstream. That is the bullwhip effect from the textbook. And now, when everyone — including the institutions who ought to be coldest — has bought "compute demand is infinite" and is expanding in the same direction, the upstream supply curve is stacking at a near-superexponential rate. That capacity won't vanish because you called it "AI-essential"; it is pouring, with its own hands, the concave position — no amount of optimistic demand can outrun the capacity now roaring in, and any marginal softening in demand gets amplified in reverse into an avalanche in price.

Seen through the tide, it's clearer still. This round of optical earnings is riding the flood tide of a wave of compute capex — hyperscalers pouring money into data centers over the same stretch of time, that tide of credit washing over the entire chain and lifting the profit of every link to a historic high. But the nature of a tide is that it must ebb. Capex is no perpetual-motion machine; it is a build cycle with a beginning and an end. When these data centers are finished, when the same players shift from "accelerating build-out" to "digesting depreciation," the tide withdraws — and the links furthest upstream, the ones the bullwhip amplified hardest, drain fastest of all. Price was never the water line hung in mid-air; price is the tide itself — on the way up you think you're standing tall, and only on the way down do you find it was the water that moved.

One layer deeper: the most dangerous thing about that 711% is that it presses the market's compute to its lowest. Early in a trend, price is genuinely computing — computing the true slope of compute demand, the pace of technological diffusion, how long this business can actually last; the pricing then is distributed, clear-eyed, aggregating countless judgments into high compute. But at this moment of "results delivered, growth rate at a record," price computes only one thing: because it rose, because the earnings are this fierce, it will keep rising. Even institutional cheerleading, even the grand "AI is essential" narrative, stop being independent judgments and become fuel fed into that self-referential machine. The instant the number is most staggering is precisely the instant the market's compute is lowest — it is no longer computing the future, only hunting reasons for yesterday's rise to keep rising.

So don't let that dazzling growth rate blind you. The real margin of safety was never "is the story big enough," "is the growth rate fierce enough" — it is **whether the pace of realized growth can outrun the pace of capacity release** — and at the moment everyone charges in holding 711%, that race is already decided. Whether it rises, time will call; but the risk-reward you participate at, this moment, is something you can compute cold, this moment. Mistake the fiercest growth rate for the steadiest growth, and every cent of premium you pay is exactly the stretch of downside someone else is holding open for you.
