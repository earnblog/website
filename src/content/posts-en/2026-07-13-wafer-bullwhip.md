As of July 12, the semiconductor wafer index is up nearly 100% year to date, names across the group taking turns limit-up, doubling in relay, the momentum fierce. The market's account of it shrinks to a single line: AI demand is too hot, wafers can't keep up, the price rise is structural — this is growth, not a cycle. The reason to buy shrinks to a line too — it's rising, and it'll rise more. The whole group's pricing rests on one consensus almost no one re-checks: the shortage is real, and the rise is permanent.

But step back once and you see that for an asset already doubled this year, the real question is never "how much higher." It is a colder one: buying it today, what are you actually buying — the upside, or the downside? A price already up 100% — how much un-exhausted upside is left above its head, and how much downside, that no one wants to look at, has opened beneath its feet? The answer has nothing to do with whether wafers are scarce, and everything to do with whether your purchase is picking up a bargain or catching a falling knife.

**A doubled wafer price rises not on the shortage but on the shadow of hoarding — on expectation amplified layer by layer down the supply chain, a bullwhip cracked out to its tip.** This is the bullwhip effect: end demand moves an inch, and through distribution, modules, fabs, and wafers, each stacking inventory and safety margin, the signal reaching the top of the chain is amplified into "acute shortage." The wafer sits at the very top, exactly the tip where the whip snaps hardest. Once prices rise, downstream hoards in panic, over-orders, and paper demand explodes in an instant — so it looks scarcer, so prices rise again. Rising prices manufacture shortage, and shortage manufactures rising prices. This is a machine of positive feedback with a throttle and no brake; what makes it spin is not more chips consumed, but every link hoarding the next one's fear.

Lay out the early and late of a trend and you see which stretch we stand in. Early on, wafers rise on a mix of real forces — AI demand, restocking, genuinely tight capacity — and the price computes a real gap. By the late stage, the many forces exit, and all that remains is one self-loop: rise, so hoard; hoard, so scarce; scarce, so rise. The market no longer computes what a wafer is worth, only who takes the next baton. Today's spectacle of "doubling in relay" and "names limit-up in turn" is precisely that late-stage signature: compute compressed, dimension-reduced into price chasing price. The wildest moment of the rise is also the moment this machine's power to compute the future runs lowest.

So the lattice of risk versus reward has quietly flipped at the point of doubling. The upside has been heavily pre-spent by the story of "permanent shortage, structural growth" — to carry it higher now needs an even wilder expectation of price hikes, a super-exponential imagination. The downside, meanwhile, opens along two things that will surely come. One: end demand need only flatten at the margin, and the inventory stacked layer on layer releases in reverse — the whip snaps back, a destocking fiercer than the rise. Two: the moment the price-hike logic is falsified, all that holds the price up is price itself. To chase in here is to buy the inverse of a cigar butt — a cigar butt is limited upside, sealed downside; a chased double is capped upside, open downside, and what your hand reaches for is a coal burning red. And a double built on price hikes is simple interest, one-off; real growth should be endogenous, compounding. To mistake the simple interest of one hoarding wave for the compounding of permanent growth is the most expensive misreading in a rally like this.

People carry an instinct to mistake "rising hardest" for "safest," as if the rise itself were the moat. The opposite is true: every further tick after a double worsens the risk-reward — the upside thinner, the downside thicker. The market will, in the end, relearn to tell a real shortage from the shadow of hoarding — only, the moment it does, is usually right after it has paid, in hard cash, for that shadow.

<p class="kicker">In a doubled price, the half that climbed is someone else's imagination; and what you must carry alone, once the imagination clears out, is the half that remains.</p>
