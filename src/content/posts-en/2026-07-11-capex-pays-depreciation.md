Demand can stop. Depreciation cannot.

The most reassuring number in the market this year is the "confirmed order." The upstream discounts two years of compute demand into a near-trillion-dollar backlog and keeps telling the market that demand is locked, not imagined. Meanwhile another number is spoken of deliberately lightly: the industry now pours close to half a trillion dollars a year of capex into AI, and the revenue that actually monetizes on the enterprise side is still only a fraction of it. Most people read the two as one sentence: demand this certain, spending this justified. But pull the time axis one notch longer and something surfaces that every backlog covers up — it is not on the demand side, it is on the cost side, and it does not stop for a single day.

That thing is depreciation.

**Capex bets on demand lasting forever; depreciation only reads the calendar.** This is the crack most central to — and most avoided in — this entire AI arms race. When a company turns tens of billions of cash into a hall of accelerators, it is making a very old wager: that the hardware, before it is scrapped, will sell enough compute to earn the money back with interest. The wager has two legs, one pointing up and one pointing down. The upward leg is demand — it must keep growing, and fast enough. The downward leg is depreciation — from the first second the machines draw power, their book value begins sliding toward zero: three to four years, gone. The two legs are utterly different in nature. Whether demand arrives depends on thousands of customers' budgets, moods, and competitive maps next year — full of uncertainty. Whether depreciation happens depends on no one. It is a verdict co-signed by physics and accounting, and it does not budge.

This is why "a near-trillion confirmed backlog" is far more comforting than it is informative. An order is an IOU on the upward leg — it answers "how much do people intend to buy over the next two years." It says not one word about the downward leg: "how much will this hardware depreciate before its compute is ever bought." Worse, the two legs are staggered in time. Capex is cash spent now — certain, irreversible. Demand is revenue that *may* land later — conditional, and able to shrink at any moment. You are betting a cost nailed down today against a revenue that only reveals itself tomorrow, and running between them is that depreciation curve that never gives a discount.

The bullwhip effect widens the crack into a chasm. Even if end AI demand merely slows from "fast growth" to "moderate growth" — note, still growing — the change propagates up the supply chain layer by layer, and at the very top, at compute capex, it can show up as orders halved and expansion slammed to a stop. Because the upstream never serves demand itself; it serves demand's **derivative**. It expands against the *rate* of growth, so once the rate peaks, even with total volume at a record, what the upstream feels is winter. That is why the gap of "half a trillion in capex, a fraction in enterprise revenue" is so dangerous: it is not a static valuation discount but a dynamic gap that only a persistently above-expectation demand can fill — and that tears wide open the instant growth eases even slightly.

Read it forward, read it backward — it is the same thing. On the way up: compute is scarce → whoever hoards more wins → a capex race → hardware purchases surge → upstream revenue at records → valuations at optimistic multiples → funding costs near zero → so hoard chips harder. The chain is self-reinforcing; hoarding chips itself manufactures the *appearance* of scarcity, and the appearance in turn licenses the next round of hoarding. But once demand's growth rate peaks, the same chain runs backward untouched: compute glut → whoever hoarded most eats depreciation first → capex freezes → hardware orders cliff → upstream revenue falls → valuations cut to pessimistic multiples → funding window shuts → so dump the standing inventory of compute and turn glut into a price war. **On the way up, hoarding manufactures scarcity; on the way down, depreciation manufactures glut.** No one is manipulating anything. The system is computing on its own, and it computes more accurately than anyone — only it is computing the depreciation account three or four years out, while the market is reading today's backlog.

So it all lands on the one thing that truly matters: risk-reward. For whoever foots this round of capex, the upside is to believe AI monetization will forever outrun hardware depreciation, to believe this capacity build is unlike every capacity build in history that ended in glut, to believe the demand leg can keep running ahead of the depreciation leg. The downside is that demand's growth rate need only return to "normal," and depreciation catches monetization first, turning the compute booked on the balance sheet today into a writedown on tomorrow's income statement. The upside must be propped up by "this time is different"; the downside needs only time to run its course. **The upside needs faith; the downside needs only the calendar.** A payoff that requires faith to be realized and only time to lose you money is not a good payoff, however full the order book.

At bottom, compute can be a faith, but depreciation cannot. You can talk yourself into demand growing forever; you cannot talk a wafer of accelerators into aging more slowly. Capex bets on a story about the future; depreciation collects an account about physics. The story can be retold; the account is never re-entered. The real counterparty of this entire race was never the rival next door hoarding chips too. It is the depreciation curve no one can change and no one will look at — it attends no roadshow, reads no research, believes in nothing. It simply, punctually, every day, writes down yesterday's certainty by one more notch.

<p class="kicker">However thick the order book, it cannot hold back the pendulum of depreciation. Demand can change its mind anytime; depreciation, from the second of power-on, never negotiates again.</p>
