An expectation of rate cuts is not a forecast about the Fed. It is a requirement of asset prices.

Put two numbers side by side. On June 17, the Federal Open Market Committee decided to maintain the target range for the federal funds rate at 3.50% to 3.75%. The midpoint of that range is 3.625%. In the Summary of Economic Projections released the same day, the median participant projection for the federal funds rate at end-2026 was 3.8%; the central tendency was 3.6% to 4.1%, and the full range of projections ran from 3.4% to 4.4%.

Align them: current midpoint 3.625%, median year-end projection 3.8%. **The median sits above the current rate.** On the Fed's own table, the median points to a hike, not a cut.

And the market has been pricing "they will eventually cut" for most of a year.

That is the problem, and the problem is not forecast accuracy. **The market is never forecasting the Fed. The market is shopping for a premise its own valuations require.**

To see it, take the discount rate out of its textbook position.

Any asset whose value sits in the future — the unprofitable growth name, the compute capex that only pays off in a decade, the metal and the digital asset that throw off no coupon — is worth some future sum divided by a discount rate. The smaller the denominator, the more violently the numerator is magnified. And the magnification is not linear: the further out the cash flow, the more sensitive it is to the denominator. For something whose value mostly lands five years out, a one-point move in the discount rate moves the valuation by low double digits. **The more an asset leans on the future, the less it is a bet on the future and the more it is a bet on the denominator.**

So once a rally has pushed valuations to a certain altitude, something becomes unavoidable: that price must find a denominator that explains it. **It is not that a view on rates comes first and pricing follows. The price comes first, and the rate it requires is reverse-engineered afterward.**

Then the loop closes.

High valuations need a low discount rate to be justified → the market forms and reinforces an expectation of eventual cuts → that expectation itself becomes the support for valuations → valuations go higher → higher valuations require still more cutting to be justified. Around the circle, back to the start — and each lap, the appetite for cuts is larger.

This is textbook reflexivity: expectation moves price, price reinforces expectation. Its most dangerous property is that nobody inside the loop can feel the loop. Everyone sincerely believes they are doing fundamental work — I looked at the inflation path, I looked at employment, I computed the real rate, I concluded cuts. But when tens of thousands of people "independently" reach the same conclusion, and that conclusion happens to be exactly what their positions require, it is not analysis. It is need. **A person can barely distinguish "I judge this will happen" from "I need this to happen" — because in the head, both thoughts arrive in the same tone of voice.**

Now back to the dot plot. It did something rather ungracious: it swapped out the market's denominator.

Not through a surprise hike, not through a hawkish speech, but by laying the participants' own projections on paper — median 3.8%, central tendency 3.6% to 4.1%, full range 3.4% to 4.4%. Note the shape of that distribution. Its lower bound of 3.4% sits barely a notch below today's midpoint, while its upper bound of 4.4% sits more than seventy basis points above it. **The distribution is skewed upward.** Even on the reading most flattering to the bulls, the room below is thin and the tail above is long.

That is what the risk-reward grid looks like in the rates cell, and it propagates layer by layer into every asset leaning on the future.

Upside: requires the inflation path to keep softening, employment to weaken visibly, and both to happen fast enough to move the whole table while the officials' median already points the other way. That is a string of conditions that must *all* hold.

Downside: requires only that the median stay where it is. **It needs no bad news. It needs merely the absence of good news.**

This is the canonical shape of having paid for the best case: the upside needs a chain of conditions to coincide, the downside needs only the status quo. And once a cut is already inside the price, an actual cut delivers not a rally but a shrug — "at last." No cut delivers the entire disappointment. **You drew the best outcome forward and left every other outcome on your own side of the ledger.**

Mirror the loop and its early and late phases are equally legible.

Early: rates genuinely are in a downtrend, and the expectation of cuts is held up by several real inputs — inflation falling, employment softening, the central bank's language easing. Betting on cuts here is betting on a process already underway. Expectation and reality confirm each other, and the money made comes from being right.

Late: the inputs go dark one by one. Inflation stops falling, employment does not break, the language tightens. But the expectation of cuts does not go dark with them, because by now it no longer lives on those inputs. **It lives on valuations. Valuations need it, so it persists.** Betting on cuts here is no longer betting on a process. It is a position defending itself. The money was made long ago; what remains is the explanation.

In that sense, reading Friday's globally synchronized, cross-asset, indiscriminate selling as "some headline scared the market" reads too shallow. What was actually happening was a recalibration of the denominator. When most of the high-multiple assets in a market share one assumption about the denominator, then when the denominator moves they must move together — not because their fundamentals are alike, but because their prices contain the same premise. **Moving in unison does not reveal correlation. It reveals a shared excuse.**

So what does one do? There is no clever answer here, only an honest exercise: **pull the premise out of the price and see whether it stands on its own.**

Ask something plain. If the rate simply sits where it is for a year and does not move, is the thing in my hands still worth what I paid? If the answer is "it would have to fall quite a bit," then the reason you hold it is not the company. It is the denominator. That is not necessarily wrong — but you should at least know what you actually own. **Many people believe they bought a company's future. What they bought was an assumption about interest rates.**

Ultimately, the Fed owes the market nothing. It made no promise and dropped no hint. It merely published a table on June 17 with a median of 3.8%. It was the market that, over the preceding months, made a commitment on the Fed's behalf that the Fed had never made, and then priced everything as though it had been given.

And this is where complex systems are least accommodating: they are entirely indifferent to human need. Price can run on need for a very long time, but at settlement the system honors only the real denominator. A person can tell a story about their position for years. The system does not listen for a second.

Real humility is admitting you cannot forecast the rate — and therefore declining to buy the prices that only work if you can.

<p class="kicker">You think you are forecasting the central bank. You are drafting an application your position needs it to approve.</p>
