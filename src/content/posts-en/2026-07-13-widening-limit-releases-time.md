On July 6 the new trading rules took effect, and the clause everyone watched was this: the daily price limit on flagged risk stocks — the ST and *ST names — widened from 5% to 10%, level with the rest of the main board. The commentary split into two opposing camps. One called it a market-oriented reform that improves price discovery and loosens the leash on risk stocks; the other fretted that it doubles the volatility of the worst names on the board, and risk with it. They sound like opposites, yet they stand on the same error — both mistake a floodgate for the risk itself.

**Widening the price limit releases neither risk nor efficiency; it releases time.** A price limit never changes what a stock is worth. It changes only the speed at which that worth gets claimed by the market. A badly overvalued stock, under a 5% cap, needs a string of limit-down days to grind its price down to where a fatal piece of news says it belongs; raise the cap to 10% and the same value clears in a day or two. Run it the other way, and a wrongly beaten-down name climbs to fair value faster too. Widen the gate and there is not one drop more water in the river, nor one drop less — all that changes is when the tide reaches shore.

In truth, a price limit is the most classic of illusions: the belief that if you cap the movement of price, you cap the risk behind it. That is trying to move an entire network with a single button. But risk never lives inside that limit — it lives in the asset itself: in the fundamentals, in whether this company still has any credit, in what it is actually worth. A gate can hold price still for a while; it cannot hold back where price finally has to go. Set the limit-down band as narrow as you like — a stock that deserves to be delisted does not become worth one cent more for it. You have only made it take longer, in a longer procession of one-tick limit days, to fall to the very same destination. Cover your ears and the bell still rings; you have only delayed the moment you hear it.

In that sense, the tighter the limit, the more distorted the price. An overvalued stock pinned to a 5% gate, hanging at the limit day after day with no one able to trade, prints a price — "down only 5%" — that is a lie: not a value the market claimed, but a number the gate hung up for the moment. The pent-up pricing pressure does not vanish; it piles up behind the gate like a dammed river. Widening the limit does not "manufacture" risk; it hands that dammed, distorted right of pricing back to the market, letting price say sooner the thing it wanted to say all along. What is truly dangerous is never that price moves fast — it is that price is held, cannot move, and lets everyone believe the risk isn't there.

So on this new rule the question to ask is not "will volatility rise" — volatility is merely the speed at which value gets realized, and speed is not risk. The question is how deep the pit inside those flagged companies actually runs. As deep as the pit goes, so goes the risk, and that is identical in a 5% world and a 10% world alike. A rule can decide how many days the pit takes to hit bottom; it cannot decide how deep the pit is. Man forever wants to tame the market with rules — and in the end, all a rule can tame is time. It cannot tame value, and it certainly cannot tame the truth.

<p class="kicker">A gate can decide when the tide reaches shore; it can never decide how high the tide finally rises.</p>
