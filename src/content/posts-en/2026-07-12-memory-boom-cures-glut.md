A line is going around the market: this round of memory-chip price hikes is an AI-driven structural transformation, and storage has finally "left the cycle behind and joined the AI core track." DRAM has risen three quarters running; houses keep marking third-quarter contract prices higher, past thirty percent, and expect the squeeze to run to 2028 at least; the leaders guide mid-year profit up tens, even hundreds of times over. All these numbers read as one conclusion: this time is different, this is not a cycle, this is growth.

But whenever an industry solemnly announces it has "left the cycle behind," history has waited quietly for it at the very next turn. Memory is not the first industry to say this, and almost certainly not the last. What deserves asking is never whether the boom can last to 2028, but a plainer, more awkward question: what disease is this fierce round of price hikes curing — and at the very same time, what is it planting?

**A price hike is never proof of demand; it is the shadow of a shortfall.** This is the common sense most buried under the excitement of rising prices. A rising price says only that today's supply cannot keep up with today's demand — it says not one word about where the gap came from or how it will close. And this gap's origin is no new story at all: it came from the last bust. Two years ago memory prices fell below cash cost, the majors cut output in unison, slashed capex, and mothballed lines — throttling future supply with their own hands. Today's squeeze is the echo of yesterday's bust. What the price hike cures is the last round's undersupply; what it proves is not that this industry has escaped the cycle, but precisely that its cycle is as punctual as ever.

Read it forward, read it backward — it is the same thing. On the way up: a gap opens → prices rise → makers reap windfalls → valuations get growth-stock multiples → funding turns cheap → so they expand and hoard and lock in orders like mad → and the hoarding itself worsens today's squeeze, shouting the gap louder. The chain is self-reinforcing: rising prices manufacture the panic of "must hoard," and the panic in turn licenses the next round of hikes. But once the capacity ordered today lands together two or three years out — the physical lead time of a fab is exactly that long — the same chain runs backward untouched: glut → prices fall → makers post losses → valuations cut back to cyclical multiples → funding window shuts → so dump inventory and slash orders, turning glut into a price war. **On the way up, the price hike breeds the glut; on the way down, the glut breeds the next shortage.** No one is manipulating anything. The system computes on its own, more accurately than anyone — only it is computing the capacity account three years out, while the market is reading this quarter's price letter.

This is why "leaving the cycle behind" is a self-negating phrase. Memory's cycle never swings back because demand vanishes; it swings back because prosperity itself summons excess supply. The bullwhip effect widens the crack into a chasm: even if end AI demand merely slows from "explosive growth" to "fast growth" — note, still growing — the change propagates up the chain, server → module → wafer → equipment, and at the very top, at capacity, it can show up as orders halved. Because the upstream never serves demand itself; it serves demand's **growth rate**. Once the rate peaks, even with volume at a record, what the upstream feels is the chill of glut. The more you believe "this structural growth never turns back," the harder you expand — and the harder you fall when the pendulum returns.

So it all lands on the one thing that truly matters: risk-reward. For whoever foots the bill at these highs, the upside is to believe this time is truly unlike every capacity build in history that ended in glut, to believe AI demand's growth rate can forever outrun new capacity, to believe a price already up three quarters can climb two more years. The downside is that demand's growth rate need only return to "normal" just as the capacity ordered two or three years ago lands, and the price slides from the top of the supercycle back down the same curve to cash cost. As for those "hundred-fold" mid-year numbers — what they measure is not how strong this year is, but how wretched last year was. A staggering year-on-year has as its denominator the trough of the last bust; the lower the base, the more terrifying the growth — and that is exactly what a cyclical stock looks like at a top. **The upside needs you to believe "this time is different"; the downside needs only the capacity to land on schedule.** A payoff that requires faith to be realized and only time to swing back is not a good payoff, however loud the price letter.

At bottom, you can give memory a sexier name and call it "the AI core track," but you cannot swap out the pendulum inside it. The cycle is not memory's enemy; the cycle is memory itself — bust births boom, boom births bust, and it needs no one's permission. The real party pricing this rally was never the rival next door locking in orders too. It is the capacity-landing curve no one can change and no one will look at: it reads no research, believes no narrative, and simply, punctually, gives back every notch of today's shortage.

<p class="kicker">The boom can cure last cycle's bust, but it cannot cure this industry's memory; every time it cries "I have left the cycle behind," it is signing, in advance, the date of the next glut.</p>
