Overnight into today, the memory chain caved in. SK Hynix has round-tripped nearly 40% off its June high, more than 20% in two sessions, and yesterday it printed its largest single-day drop on record in Seoul — over one hundred and fifty billion dollars of market value gone in a day; Micron, Western Digital and SanDisk followed into a pre-market bloodbath. What crushed it wasn't a demand print. It was one offhand line from Morgan Stanley — "memory is, fundamentally, a cyclical commodity." That line went in like a needle and popped the word hanging over all of AI hardware. Even Michael Burry piled on, filing this round of giant-led, frenzied capacity expansion as one of the markers that "the AI investment cycle is beginning to turn."

But the harder it fell, the harder that word got summoned back to hold the line: **supercycle**. In the notes, on the roadshows, in the reassurance blasts, the same comfort on repeat — "this round is different from the old memory cycles; it's an AI-driven supercycle, the demand is structural, it won't swing wildly like before." As if those two syllables put a policy of insurance on the cycle, promoting it from a mean-reverting mortal commodity into a new species that only climbs. **But a supercycle is never a cycle grown longer or stronger; it is what a cycle writes for itself once it has topped and begun to need defending.** A genuinely healthy cycle needs no adjective.

To see this, you first have to break open the most expensive sentence in markets: "this time is different." Open the record before any cyclical top and you find the same move: right when price has climbed past what the old logic can explain, the market obligingly grows a new noun to argue "why this rise won't stop." In 1999 it was the "New Economy," the internet voiding every old valuation ruler. In 2007 it was the "commodity supercycle," China's demand cast as inexhaustible. Today it is the "AI supercycle." The names get louder each time, but the mechanism beneath them has never changed — it is still the oldest positive feedback loop: demand expectations pull forward expansion, expansion drives up prices, higher prices reinforce the "infinite demand" expectation, which summons still more expansion. This is not a new law of physics. It is a self-reinforcing loop — it can stretch a cycle's amplitude to the extreme, but it has never once cancelled the cycle itself.

And memory happens to be the most convex of all cyclical commodities. Its supply is built in years — fabs, capacity; its demand changes face by the quarter, by the month. Between them sits an entire supply chain that magnifies a small twitch in end demand, layer by layer, into towering waves upstream — the textbook bullwhip effect. When everyone, including the institutions who should be coldest, has bought the "supercycle" and is expanding capacity in the same direction, the supply curve is stacking upward at a near-superexponential rate. That capacity will not vanish because you called it "super." It builds exactly a concave position — capped on the upside, wide open on the downside: no amount of optimistic demand can outrun the capacity now roaring in, and any marginal softening in demand gets amplified in reverse into an avalanche in price. A near-40% round-trip isn't an accident; it's the bill a concave position always comes to pay.

Put it in the risk-reward lattice and the picture turns colder still. An asset crowned "super" has already spent most of its upside on narrative, back in that doubling euphoria before the crash; while its downside, precisely because the vast new capacity has only just come online and is only starting to unload, is wide open. This is the worst risk-reward structure there is — a ceiling on the upside, no floor beneath. Every cent of premium you pay for "this time is different" is exactly the downside someone else is holding open for you. Picking the priciest cigar butt, and mistaking it for growth. The real margin of safety was never "the story is big enough"; it is whether the pace of realized growth can outrun the pace of capacity release — and at a moment when everyone is chanting "super," that race is already decided.

Deeper still: the "super" in supercycle sounds like the peak of computation; it is actually its exhaustion. Early in a trend, price is genuinely computing — computing the real supply gap, the slope of technological diffusion, the shape of the demand curve; there it is distributed, clear-eyed, aggregating countless judgments into high compute. But late in the trend, price computes only one thing: "because it's still rising, it will keep rising." Even institutional cheerleading, giant expansion, central-bank-grade endorsement stop being independent judgments and become fuel fed to that self-referential machine. The most frenzied moment is precisely the moment of lowest compute — the market is no longer computing the future, only hunting reasons for yesterday's rise to keep rising. The louder "supercycle" is shouted, the fewer people are still actually computing.

So don't be fooled by the word. A cycle will not stop mean-reverting because you gave it a grand name; the tide, rising and falling, has never heard of "super." A strength that must be named, propped up, and defended over and over to stand was never real strength — real strength delivers in silence and needs no adjective for courage. When a cycle starts being solemnly called "super," that is not the hour of its coronation; it is the hour it began arranging its own affairs.

<p class="kicker">"Supercycle" is not a cycle's coronation but the epitaph it carved in advance — the bigger the letters, the closer the curtain.</p>
