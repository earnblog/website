The deepest greed man holds toward the future is not the wish to own it, but the wish to put a precise price on it.

The second half has barely opened and the targets already blanket the screen. Gold: one house calls for a range around forty-one hundred, another lifts its third- and fourth-quarter averages toward forty-five hundred, a third simply shouts six thousand — the numbers sit nearly fifty percent apart. Index levels, a given sector's TAM out to 2035, the same: every shop hands over a figure precise to the last digit and dressed in the gravest ceremony. The less anyone can truly reckon a thing, the more urgently people give it a definite price — as if, so long as the number is precise enough and authoritative enough, uncertainty itself had been tamed.

**To put a price on the future is to nail a still-growing complex system to a single number — and that is the oldest arrogance of man.** The future is not a point lying there waiting to be discovered; it is a field still emerging: grown by the joint force of countless people's judgments, actions, reversals, and re-judgments, rewriting itself in every instant. The market, the price of gold, a nation's growth — none is an answer some person or model can compute in advance. Each is the result of everyone's highest compute converging, contending, crystallizing bit by bit in time. To anchor it with a number precise to the last digit is, at bottom, to press an entire sea flat with one finger — and what you pin is only a bubble on the surface of this moment, never the tide itself.

In that sense, the more precise a forecast, the more arrogant it tends to be. Precision means the forecaster believes his own compute, alone, can outweigh the aggregate compute of everyone in the system; believes that field, still growing, will halt obediently at the point he marked and wait for him. But the deepest temperament of a complex system is exactly its reflexivity: the instant your judgment is spoken, believed, turned into a trade, it has already rewritten the very path it described. The act of forecasting the future is itself a disturbance to the future. So those rigorous-looking targets are less a measurement of what is coming than a projection of the forecaster's present conviction — they mark not what gold will be worth years out, but how badly the one pricing it wants, today, to grasp certainty.

True humility is to admit you cannot out-compute the whole system. It offers no pretend-precise point, only a direction, a range, an honest confidence — and stands ready at every moment to be proven wrong. This is not incapacity; it is precisely the clear sight of one's place in that vast distributed computer, no more than one of tens of millions of nodes. You may take part in the computing, may bet, may revise — but you hold no right to declare the answer on the system's behalf ahead of time. To admit this is not surrender but clarity: only when "I forecast" becomes "I wager, and stand ready to be wrong" does a person finally stand as an equal to the complex system.

So each time you meet a target stated with iron certainty, what merits notice is usually not the number but the self behind it that refuses to lose. Man forever wants to nail a coordinate onto the unknown, so he can sleep soundly in the chaos; but the future is future precisely because it refuses to be priced in advance by anyone. To study a world like this ought to be a pilgrimage — the closer you draw, the more unfathomable it shows itself, the lower you bow your head. The arrogant do the reverse: the less they can reckon, the louder the precise number they shout, using the posture of certainty to paper over the substance of uncertainty. What is truly scarce in this world is never the one bold enough to forecast the future — it is the one brave enough to admit he cannot out-reckon it.

<p class="kicker">The future accepts no price tag, only awe — the harder you try to nail it into a number, the more it uses the depth of a whole sea to show you that you are only a bubble.</p>
